The Digital Divide

In the 21st century, access to the internet and digital tools is no longer a luxury — it is a fundamental enabler of education, economic opportunity, healthcare, and civic participation. Yet billions of people around the world remain disconnected, creating a widening gap known as the digital divide. This divide disproportionately affects people in low-income households, rural communities, and developing nations, directly deepening social and economic inequality — a core concern of SDG 10: Reduced Inequalities.

2.6B
People worldwide still have no access to the internet, according to the International Telecommunication Union (ITU, 2023).

The digital divide is not simply about owning a device — it encompasses access to reliable internet, the skills to use digital tools effectively, and the ability to afford ongoing connectivity. Without addressing all three layers, inequality only deepens.

Infrastructure & Connectivity

One of the most significant barriers to digital access is the lack of physical infrastructure. In many rural and remote regions, broadband cables, mobile towers, and reliable power grids simply do not exist. Governments and private companies have historically prioritised high-density urban areas, leaving vast populations underserved.

Initiatives such as satellite internet services, community Wi-Fi hubs, and government-subsidised broadband expansion are beginning to close this gap. However, progress remains uneven, and without sustained investment, rural and low-income communities will continue to be left behind.

"Universal, affordable internet access is as essential as roads and electricity for human development in the modern era." — UN Broadband Commission

Affordability Barriers

Even where internet infrastructure exists, the cost of connectivity and devices can be prohibitive for low-income households. In many developing countries, a monthly mobile data plan can represent a significant portion of daily income. Similarly, the price of smartphones, laptops, and tablets places them out of reach for millions of families.

40%
Of people in low-income countries say cost is the primary reason they do not use the internet (Alliance for Affordable Internet, 2022).

Addressing affordability requires a multi-pronged approach: reducing device costs through refurbishment programmes, introducing tiered or subsidised data plans, and ensuring public access points such as libraries and community centres provide free internet. Organisations like ALIGN advocate for these inclusive solutions to ensure that digital participation is not determined by income.

Digital Literacy & Education

Access to a device and a connection is only meaningful if people have the skills to use them. Digital literacy — the ability to find, evaluate, and use information online, communicate safely, and navigate digital services — is a critical but often overlooked component of bridging the digital divide.

Many marginalised communities, including older adults, women in conservative regions, and people with limited formal education, face significant barriers to developing digital skills. Without targeted literacy programmes, even providing devices and connectivity will not fully close the inequality gap.

Community-based digital training centres, multilingual online learning platforms, and peer mentorship programmes are all proven strategies for building digital confidence. Inclusive design — ensuring that platforms are accessible to users with disabilities and available in multiple languages — is equally essential to making the digital world truly open to all.

ALIGN promotes platforms that are multilingual, user-friendly, and accessible — so that digital participation is not limited by language or ability.
